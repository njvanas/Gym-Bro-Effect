## Verified principles (A/B only)

1. **"Total annihilation" is Gaspari's own term for his competition-era training mindset** — training "to battle, to war," to complete failure using advanced techniques, confirmed in his own Instagram words (Fitness Volt Tier B republish). It is **not** a DVD or officially branded program name — confirmed by the absence of any such product across his official site.
2. **"Stimulate, not annihilate" is a *separate*, earlier-career lesson Gaspari says Lee Haney taught him** — in Gaspari's own first-person blog essays (Tier A), he credits Haney with teaching him to use less weight, better form, and slower, controlled reps instead of chasing raw poundage (he was benching 525 lbs before this). The style id's name ("annihilation") and the current summary's "stimulate, not annihilate" framing are drawing on **two different, chronologically distinct parts of Gaspari's own story** — worth clarifying in any future rewrite so the two aren't conflated as one teaching.
3. **Failure/recovery cycling, in his own words (Tier B, verbatim IG captions via Fitness Volt):** train a muscle to complete failure with advanced intensity techniques (supersets, pre-exhaustion, drop sets, forced reps, negatives) — but only about **twice a month**, not every week. The week after a high-intensity/failure week, train the same muscle **moderately, for the pump**, to allow recovery. Muscle grows during rest, not during the training itself.
4. **Training frequency cap, his own words:** "the best system is working out four days, a maximum of five days" per week — training every day doesn't allow enough recovery.
5. **Pre-exhaustion is a specifically named technique** he coaches today (official brand content): isolate the target muscle to fatigue before a compound movement, most concretely demonstrated for shoulders (lateral/front raise → press → rear-delt finisher). Recommended frequency: once or twice a week for a given muscle, not more.
6. **Modern self-reflection (Generation Iron interview, Tier B):** Gaspari says his own generation trained too far past exhaustion (he tore a pec), and that today's bodybuilders understand recovery/rest better — reinforcing that his current coaching voice leans toward "train hard, then truly rest," not constant annihilation.

## Draft summary (A/B only)

> Rich Gaspari's coaching draws on two distinct chapters of his own career, in his own words: (1) his self-described competition-era mindset of training "to battle, to war... to total annihilation or total failure," using advanced techniques (drop sets, forced reps, supersets, pre-exhaustion) to force muscle breakdown; and (2) an earlier lesson from training partner Lee Haney to "stimulate, not annihilate" — trade brute weight (he once benched 525 lbs) for slower, controlled reps taken to true failure with better form. Today's public coaching voice reconciles both: train a muscle hard to complete failure with advanced techniques only about twice a month, then follow with a moderate "pump" week the next week so the muscle actually recovers and grows; cap training at 4, max 5, days a week. Pre-exhaustion (isolation before compound) is his most concretely documented current technique, published with a full shoulder-day template on his brand's official blog.

## Draft principles (A/B only)

1. "Total annihilation" describes Gaspari's own competition-era failure-training mindset — not a branded program, DVD, or split name.
2. Separately, and earlier in his career, Lee Haney taught him "stimulate, not annihilate": less weight, stricter form, full control on every rep — worth keeping distinct from principle 1 rather than merging them.
3. Train a muscle to complete failure (with advanced techniques: drop sets, forced reps, supersets, pre-exhaustion, negatives) only about twice a month.
4. The week following a failure/high-intensity week, train the same muscle moderately for blood flow and recovery — growth happens during rest, not during the workout.
5. Cap frequency at 4, maximum 5, training days per week; daily training doesn't allow adequate recovery.
6. Pre-exhaust isolation work before compounds, especially for lagging body parts like shoulders — but no more than once or twice a week per muscle.
