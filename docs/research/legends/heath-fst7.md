## Verified FST‑7 protocol (Tier A/B only)

**Origin.** Created by coach Hany Rambod in 2007, first used to intensify Phil Heath's training (M&F/FLEX, Tier B). Jay Cutler also used it late in his career (same source).

**Core structure (Rambod, in his own words, Tier A via the SimplyShredded republish of his `fst-7.com` article; numbers cross‑confirmed by M&F, Tier B):**
- Do 3–4 straight/heavy working sets of most exercises for a body part first, in the 8–12 rep zone, with normal rest (M&F says 1–3 min).
- Finish the body part with **one** isolation/machine/cable exercise for **seven sets ("sevens")**, 8–12 reps (Rambod's own copy; M&F narrows this to 10–12), resting only **30–45 seconds** between the seven sets.
- Keep the load constant across the seven sets if possible; it's fine to drop the weight once or twice to stay in range, rarely to increase it (Rambod, Tier A).
- Sip water and hold a brief flex/isometric contraction during the short rest (M&F/FLEX interview with Heath, Tier B).
- **Do not** use sevens on heavy free-weight compounds (squat, deadlift) — they don't isolate the target muscle and don't hold up technically at fast turnover. Prefer machines/cables (Rambod, Tier A).
- Sevens go **last** for that body part, after the heavy straight sets — never first (Rambod, Tier A).
- If short on multiple body parts in one session, you'll do "two or more" seven-set finishers that day (Rambod, Tier A).

**Frequency:**
- Large muscle groups are generally trained with FST‑7 **once a week** — Rambod notes FST‑7 is "too traumatic" to repeat more often; he cites Heath being sore for 4 days after an FST‑7 back session (Tier A).
- Smaller/stubborn parts (arms, calves) can be hit with sevens **twice a week** (Tier A).
- Phil Heath, in his own words (thebarbell.com, Tier B): he used sevens "for every bodypart except arms" because arms were already his strong point — a useful nuance that FST‑7 usage is individualized, not a rigid rule.

**Rambod's own sample split (Tier A, geared toward someone prioritizing stubborn arms — he explicitly frames this as one of "many other variations"):**

| Day | Focus |
|---|---|
| 1 | Biceps & triceps, calves |
| 2 | Legs |
| 3 | OFF |
| 4 | Chest & triceps |
| 5 | Back & calves |
| 6 | Shoulders & biceps |
| 7 | OFF |

**Exercise selection for the "7" (Rambod's own list, Tier A):**
- Back width: machine/cable pullover
- Back thickness: chest-supported seated row machine
- Chest: pec deck / pec-flye machine, cable crossover
- Shoulders: machine lateral raise
- Quads: leg extension, leg press
- Hamstrings: seated/lying leg curl
- Biceps: EZ-bar curl, machine curl, cable curl
- Triceps: cable pushdown, overhead cable extension, skull crusher (advanced)
- Calves: standing/seated raise, alternated with leg-press calf raise

## Draft summary (A/B sources only)

> FST‑7 (Fascia Stretch Training‑Seven) was created by coach Hany Rambod in 2007 and popularized through Phil Heath (and later Jay Cutler): after 3–4 heavy straight sets of most lifts in the 8–12 rep range, finish the body part with one isolation/machine/cable exercise for seven sets at 8–12 reps (M&F narrows to 10–12) with only 30–45 seconds rest, holding load constant where possible. Large muscle groups get FST‑7 once a week; arms/calves can take it twice — though Heath himself skipped sevens on arms specifically because they were already his strong point. Splits are individualized; Rambod's own published sample is a stubborn-arms-focused 6-day rotation, not a fixed Olympia calendar.

## Draft principles (A/B only)

1. Finish a body part with sevens on an isolation, machine, or cable movement — never on heavy free-weight compounds like squat or deadlift.
2. Sevens = 7 sets, ~8–12 reps (M&F: 10–12), 30–45s rest, same weight when possible; hold a brief flex/stretch during rest and sip water.
3. Everything before the sevens is normal straight-set training: 3–4 sets, 8–12 reps, 1–3 minutes rest.
4. Sevens go last for the body part, after the heavy work — not first.
5. Large muscle groups: once/week with FST‑7 (soreness/recovery cost is real — Rambod cites Heath being sore 4 days after an FST‑7 back session). Arms/calves: twice/week is fine, but individualize — Heath himself skipped sevens on arms.
6. If training multiple body parts in a session, expect two or more seven-set finishers that day.
