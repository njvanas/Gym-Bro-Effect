## Verified Claims (Tier A/B only)

1. **The three-way Pull/Legs/Push split, and its use for the 1979 and 1982 Olympia preps, is directly Zane's own statement** (A1, mirrored A2/A3) — matches `styles.json`'s summary exactly.
2. **The split has (at least) two historically distinct versions**, confirmed in Zane's own words (A9): a pre-1978 six-day "chest&back / legs / delts&arms" version done twice weekly (closer in spirit to Arnold's contemporaneous Encyclopedia split), and the post-1978 3-on/1-off "back-biceps-forearms / legs / chest-shoulders-triceps" version that A1 and `styles.json` describe. `styles.json`'s summary only reflects the second, later version — which is fine for scope, but the first version is a legitimate, sourced gap if the entry is ever expanded historically.
3. **The 5-5-5-6 recovery cycle is Zane's own, explicitly *retrospective* recommendation** ("If I were to do that again, I'd do a 5, 5, 5, 6 day cycle... this is something I recently thought up"), not something he actually used during his competitive career — `styles.json`'s phrasing ("Zane now prefers... so you grow between sessions") is accurate to this nuance and should be preserved if the entry is edited.
4. **Concrete, quotable rest-interval and stretch-duration numbers exist and are not yet in `styles.json`**: ~3 min ± 30 sec rest during growth-phase pyramids (A5); ~1 min during pre-contest "quality training" via partner-paced sets (A5); 15–20 second stretches per rest period, roughly ten stretches per body part worked (A5, A7, A8).
5. **"Never train upper body two days in a row" is an explicit, named safety rule** in Zane's own words (A4), not an inference — a good candidate line for `principles` if the entry is revised, since it's more specific and falsifiable than the current "Moderate loads, strict form, inter-set stretching."
6. **Daily ab work (400+ reps, up to 1,000/day pre-contest) is confirmed in two independent first-person sources** (A1's Growth Program article and A9's Flex interview), both citing similar-but-not-identical numbers — A1 implies gradual buildup toward 1,000 total reps/day by contest time; A9 says "a good 400 and upward... up to as many as 1,000 reps a day" pre-contest. These are consistent, not conflicting.

## Conflicts & Gaps

- `styles.json`'s current `splitOverview` and `summary` describe only the later (post-1978) 3-on/1-off Pull/Legs/Push version. The earlier six-day/twice-weekly three-way split Zane describes in the 2008 Flex interview (A9) is a real, sourced, distinct chapter of his training history that isn't reflected — not an error, just an incompleteness if historical depth is ever wanted.
- Two of Zane's own books — *The Workouts – Personal Training Diaries* (180+ logged workouts over 40 years) and *Zane Bodybuilding Manual* (436 pages) — are referenced repeatedly on frankzane.com as containing the full underlying detail, but neither was purchased/read in this pass (both are commercial, paywalled Kindle/print products). Treat anything beyond what's quoted on the free blog/interview pages as unverified until one of these books is actually sourced.
- No conflicts were found between A1/A2/A3 (identical republished text) or between A1 and A9 (different eras, not contradictory accounts of the same era) — Zane's sourcing is unusually clean and internally consistent for this exercise.

## Draft Copy (Tier A/B only)

> Frank Zane's Growth Program is a three-way split — Pull (back, biceps, forearms, abs), Legs (thighs, calves, abs), Push (chest, shoulders, triceps, abs) — that he used for his 1979 and 1982 Mr. Olympia preparations, training three days in a row on a 3-on/1-off cycle. Earlier in his career (pre-1978), he'd trained a different, six-day-a-week three-way split (chest&back / legs / delts&arms, each twice weekly) at much higher volume. Reflecting on the 3-on/1-off years afterward, he found he became overtrained after about a month and has since recommended a 5-5-5-6 cycle instead (three training days out of five, repeated three times, then three training days out of six once, then repeat) to allow more recovery between sessions. Within a workout, he paces rest by goal: roughly 3 minutes (±30 seconds) between sets while pyramiding weight up during growth-phase training, dropping to about a minute during pre-contest "quality training." He stretches the working muscle 15–20 seconds after every set (about ten stretches per body part across a session), keeps negatives slower than positives, and — as an explicit injury-prevention rule — never trains upper body on back-to-back days.
