## 6. Draft summary + principles + guidelines (grounded only in A/B sources)

### Summary (draft)
Jay Cutler's competitive-era training, documented on his own DVD *One Step Closer* and in direct interviews, centered on high volume without pushing to failure: roughly 20 working sets per body part (up to 30 for back), spread across 3–4 sets each of 5–6 exercises, in an 8–12 rep zone for most of his career (tightening to 10–12 on big body parts, 12–15 on arms/calves, after 2004). He explicitly avoided training to failure and skipped advanced intensity techniques like drop sets or forced reps, preferring straight sets and pure volume to "get the muscle full, damage the tissue, and get out of the gym." In retirement, Jay has publicly revised this down to about 12 working sets per body part (4 exercises × 3 sets) to match his recovery needs.

### Principles (draft)
- Aim for ~20 working sets per body part in competitive-style training (his stated career standard); back can run higher, up to ~30 sets.
- Build volume from 3–4 sets across 5–6 exercises, varying angle more than load.
- Work mostly in the 8–12 rep range; tighten to 10–12 for big body parts and 12–15 for arms/calves as you get more advanced.
- Never train to failure; skip forced reps and drop sets — straight sets, full control, every rep.
- Train back twice weekly with a width day (pulldowns/cables) and a thickness day (rows/deadlifts).
- In long-term/recovery-focused training, ~12 quality sets per body part (4 exercises × 3 sets) is a legitimate, Jay-endorsed lower-volume alternative.

### Guidelines (draft)
- **Training days/week:** not confirmed from A/B sources in this pass — flag as unverified rather than asserting a specific rotation.
- **Working-set protocol:** ~20 sets per body part (competitive era) via 3–4 sets × 5–6 exercises; excludes warm-ups/"feel sets." Recovery-era alternative: ~12 sets via 4 exercises × 3 sets.
- **Rep ranges:** 8–12 general (career-wide), narrowing to 10–12 for big body parts and 12–15 for arms/calves post-2004.
- **Intensity technique policy:** no forced reps, no drop sets, no training to failure — the opposite of the current file's `intensityTechniques: [drop-set, forced-reps, partials]`, which is not supported by any A/B source found.
