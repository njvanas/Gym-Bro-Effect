## Verified Claims (Tier A/B only)

1. **A 3-day-on/1-day-off structure is genuinely tied to Bannout's actual training history**, not just a modern SEO invention — confirmed by an actual 1981 training partner (B1), who also names the likely originator (George Turner, via a late-1970s Weider magazine article) rather than crediting Bannout as the split's inventor.
2. **The day-order within that 3-on/1-off cycle is explicitly reported as having changed over time, even by someone who trained beside him** (B1: "At one point Samir changed the split but kept the body part workouts the same... I did not like this split as well but Samir liked it"). This directly explains — and validates — `styles.json`'s existing caveat that "day order... conflicts across secondary sources": the conflict isn't just sloppy blogging, it reflects that Bannout himself used more than one day-order over his career.
3. **Bannout has a genuine, current, on-the-record training philosophy**: train by feel rather than fixed sets/reps, full range of motion taken to the point of fatigue rather than a prescribed rep target, and slow/controlled tempo especially on curls (A1). This is real and quotable but is a *philosophy* statement, not a verified sets/reps program, and is currently absent from `styles.json`'s principles/sources.
4. **Physique philosophy ("art," symmetry/proportion over size) is consistently self-stated across multiple video interviews** (A2, A5) — this supports `styles.json`'s existing line "Art and aesthetics matter as much as the weight on the bar."

## Conflicts & Gaps — documented honestly

- **No primary, page-or-timestamp-cited source for the specific numbers in `styles.json`'s workout tables was found.** Every set/rep table in circulation (C1–C4, and the pre-existing `styles.json` sources) is a Tier C aggregator reconstruction. The best Tier A/B material found (A1, B1) confirms the *shape* of Bannout's training (3-on/1-off, feel-based, no fixed rep counts, tempo-focused) but explicitly contradicts the premise of a precise sets×reps table, since Bannout himself says he doesn't count reps (A1, C1's uncredited-but-consistent quote, C3).
- **Two credible video sources exist that were not transcribed in this pass** and are the highest-value next step: DigitalMuscle's "Training with the Legends" Part 2 (A3, explicitly about training) and the 2020 exclusive interview (A4). Either could contain verbatim, on-camera Bannout training detail that would upgrade this file from "philosophy confirmed, numbers unconfirmed" to a fully Tier-A-sourced entry.
- **Wikipedia cites two 2007/2008 "Interview with the Lion of Lebanon" pieces (B2) that were not located/verified in this pass** — likely the oldest and most detailed Q&A-style interviews referenced anywhere in this research, and worth a dedicated search-engine/web-archive lookup as a follow-up.
- The IronMan eyewitness account (B1) attributes the *origin* of the 3-on/1-off system to George Turner, not Bannout — meaning even the best-sourced claim in this file is about a system Bannout *adopted*, not one he *invented*. `styles.json`'s framing ("There is no official branded 'Lion' training system") is fully consistent with this and should not be changed.
- The oft-repeated quote "Do it with feeling. Attack the weights with a vengeance..." (C1) could not be traced to a dated interview/video despite a targeted search — treat as unverified until a primary appearance is found.

## Draft Copy (Tier A/B only)

Given the gaps above, only philosophy-level copy is currently supportable from Tier A/B material — **no sets/reps table** should be presented as verified:

> Samir Bannout trained on a three-days-on, one-day-off cycle — a structure he adopted (its origin traces to a late-1970s George Turner program published in a Weider magazine, per a training partner's eyewitness account) rather than one he branded himself, and he's on record having changed its internal day-order more than once over his career. In his own words, he trains by feel rather than fixed sets and reps — taking each exercise through a full range of motion until the muscle fatigues, whether that happens at rep 14 or rep 24 — with deliberately slow, controlled tempo, especially on curls. Beyond the split, his consistent, self-stated throughline across decades of interviews is philosophical: bodybuilding as art, symmetry and proportion valued over raw mass.

## Weakest-Sourcing Verdict (across all four legends)

**Bannout (`bannout-lion`) has the weakest primary sourcing of the four legends.** Arnold has an authored book plus a dated as-told-to magazine feature; Haney has recent (2025), directly self-quoted video interviews that cross-corroborate almost verbatim; Zane has three self-published channels repeating the same first-person article plus a dated, attributed magazine Q&A. Bannout has real first-person philosophy quotes (A1–A6) and one strong eyewitness account (B1), but **every specific sets/reps table currently associated with him — including the ones already cited in `styles.json` — traces to uncredited aggregator sites, not a dated interview, book, or magazine feature.** `styles.json`'s existing hedge language ("no official branded system," "reconstructed," "not primary gospel") is the right call and should be kept or strengthened, not relaxed, until one of the two video/text interviews above is actually reviewed.
