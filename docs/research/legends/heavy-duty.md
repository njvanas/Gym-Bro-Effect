## 0. Headline finding: `mikementzer.org` provenance — RESOLVED

`00-legend-inventory.md` flagged `mikementzer.org` as "not his own site but not
a magazine either — verify provenance." That verification is now done:

- Mike Mentzer died in 2001. His family entrusted his IP to Joanne Sharkey, who
  ran **Mentzer-Sharkey Enterprises** for ~20 years.
- In 2024, Joanne's daughter Tamala Poljak (individually and as representative
  of Mentzer-Sharkey Enterprises) **officially transferred all of Mike
  Mentzer's IP rights — name, image, trademarks, and copyrights — to John
  Little and Simon Shawcross / HIT Universal Limited.** Both men were personal
  students of Mentzer; John Little co-authored two of Mentzer's own books
  (*High-Intensity Training the Mike Mentzer Way*, *The Wisdom of Mike
  Mentzer*) and was his friend for 22 years.
- `mikementzer.org` is the resulting official site, "created by the official
  custodians and sole owners of Mike Mentzer's legacy." (Source: [About —
  Mike Mentzer](https://mikementzer.org/about/), which quotes Tamala Poljak's
  full statement.)
- `Heavy Duty College` on YouTube and `hituni.com`'s Mike Mentzer course are
  run by the same two people (John Little + Simon Shawcross).

**Conclusion: `mikementzer.org`, its "Heavy Duty College" YouTube channel, and
the associated HITuni course are Tier A** under this project's own tier
definitions ("their official personal site," "their official YouTube
channel," "a brand site they personally own/founded" — Mentzer is deceased, so
his *estate's designated successor* fills that role, same as e.g. Yates'
DY Nutrition being run by his team). This resolves the biggest open question
from the inventory doc.

**Caveat worth carrying forward:** the actual long-form articles on
`mikementzer.org` are written by John Little or Simon Shawcross *about*
embedded archival Mentzer audio/video, not by Mentzer himself. Treat the
**embedded video/audio of Mentzer speaking** as Tier A (it's Mentzer's own
words), and treat the **surrounding blog prose** as Tier A-adjacent
commentary from his officially anointed successors — reliable, but a half-step
removed from Mentzer's own text. Where possible, cite what the video is
reported to contain, not the marketing framing ("this video will make you a
believer," "Get your copy today," etc. — ignore all promotional copy).

## 2. Verified facts vs. uncertain / conflicting

### Verified (Tier A, consistent across ≥2 independent Heavy Duty College /
mikementzer.org items, or a direct Mentzer quote)

- One working set per exercise, taken to true momentary muscular failure, is
  Mentzer's stated core protocol for both the Ideal Routine and the
  Consolidated Routine. (#3, #5, #8, #9, #10)
- The Ideal Routine is a 4-workout rotation: **Chest & Back → Legs → Delts &
  Arms → Legs (static hold + squat emphasis)**, matching the existing
  `styles.json` `splitOverview` exactly. (#3, #4, #5, #7)
- Ideal Routine exercise-by-exercise detail (sets/reps) is documented in full
  in source #5 — see the draft table in §4. This matches the current
  `styles.json` numbers closely.
- Pre-exhaust supersets (isolation → immediately into compound, no rest) are
  a documented, named Mentzer technique, with the pec-deck/incline-press pair
  as the flagship example. (#9, #12, #13, #14)
- The Consolidated Routine (Mentzer's final, most minimal program) is
  two alternating workouts: **A = Squat (or Leg Press) + Close-Grip Palms-Up
  Pulldown; B = Deadlift + Dips.** Reps: Squat/Leg Press 8-15, Pulldown 6-10,
  Deadlift 5-8, Dips 6-10. This is consistent across three independent Tier A
  articles (#9, #10, #11). This is a **different, more minimal program** than
  the 4-workout Ideal Routine — `styles.json` currently only documents the
  Ideal Routine, which is fine, but the two should not be conflated.
- Direct Mentzer quote on recovery scaling with strength: *"A beginner is too
  weak to overtrain, but as you grow stronger and handle 500-pound squats, you
  can overtrain very easily. You don't train more as you grow stronger, you
  train less."* (#9)
- Direct Mentzer quote on single-set logic (paraphrased transcript from his
  own recorded audio): start with one set; only add a second/third set if you
  can rationally justify it after failure of the first; "we're looking for the
  least amount necessary." (#8)
- Biography: born Nov 15, 1951, Philadelphia PA; died June 10, 2001, Rolling
  Hills, CA; 1976 Mr. America; 1978 Mr. Universe (heavyweight) with a perfect
  score; influenced by Arthur Jones' HIT. (#24, corroborated in framing by #2,
  #30) — bio facts only, not training-protocol numbers, so Wikipedia is an
  acceptable *cross-reference* here per the tier rules (Tier C is fine for
  discovery/corroboration of non-numeric-protocol facts).

### Uncertain / directly conflicting (do not silently pick one — flag it)

1. **Ideal Routine frequency: "every 3 days" vs. "every 4 days" vs. "3x/week."**
   - Source #4 and #6 (both on `mikementzer.org`) say "typically every third
     day, with at least two full days of rest between sessions."
   - Source #5 (also `mikementzer.org`) says workouts are "separated by 96
     hours (four days)."
   - Source #7 (Heavy Duty College video description) says "once-every-four-
     days."
   - Source #25 (Liftosaur, Tier C) separately claims the same routine was
     "done 3x/week" for most clients — a third, different cadence, uncited.
   - **This is an unresolved internal inconsistency even within the estate's
     own official site**, likely because different articles paraphrase
     different points in Mentzer's evolving teaching, or "every 3rd day"
     vs. "every 4th day" is being used loosely by different writers (Simon
     Shawcross vs. whoever wrote #4/#6) to mean the same "train, rest 2-3
     days" idea. `styles.json`'s current "About twice a week (train roughly
     every 4th day)" is a defensible middle position but should be phrased as
     approximate given this spread, not as a single precise number.

2. **Consolidated Routine rest interval: "once per week" vs. "7-10 days" vs.
   "4-7 days."**
   - Source #10 (the 1998 Seattle seminar article): "one workout per week...
     performed on separate weeks (A → rest 7 days → B → rest 7 days →
     repeat)."
   - Source #9: "Each workout is performed once every 7-10 days."
   - Source #11: "most often with four to seven days of rest between
     sessions."
   - These are all Tier A (`mikementzer.org`) and disagree with each other by
     as much as double. This likely reflects Mentzer's own explicit
     instruction to individualize/extend rest over time (a documented
     principle — see "verified" list above), rather than a single fixed
     number, but the site itself does not make that explicit reconciliation.
     Any future `styles.json` copy about the Consolidated Routine should state
     a range ("roughly once every 5-10+ days, individualized") rather than one
     figure, and should cite this spread.

3. **Consolidated Routine Workout B exercise list.**
   - Tier A (#9, #10, #11): Workout B = **Deadlift + Dips** only.
   - Tier C (#27, TrulyHuge, claiming to quote *Heavy Duty II* directly):
     Workout B = **Deadlift + Press Behind Neck + Standing Calf Raise**.
   - This could mean *Heavy Duty II* (1996 book) documented an earlier,
     slightly different iteration of the Consolidated concept than the 1998
     Seattle seminar's final, more minimal A/B (Squat+Pulldown /
     Deadlift+Dips) that the estate now promotes as canonical. This is
     plausible given Mentzer's own trajectory toward ever-fewer exercises, but
     **it is not verified here against an actual copy of the book** — flagged
     as a gap, not resolved.

4. **"One fixed program" framing vs. "it depends on the trainee."**
   - Source #19 (HITuni course page, Tier A) is explicit that Mentzer himself
     prescribed **three different protocols** depending on the individual:
     (a) 4 workouts/week at 6 sets/exercise, (b) 1-2 sets/muscle group every
     3-4 days, (c) two total sets once every seven days.
   - Every other article discussed above presents "the Ideal Routine" or "the
     Consolidated Routine" as if it were *the* Heavy Duty program. Source #19
     is the clearest primary-adjacent statement that Heavy Duty was a
     framework Mentzer individualized, not one single fixed routine — this
     should probably be reflected more explicitly in `styles.json`'s summary
     if the entry is ever revised (see draft in §5).

5. **Pre-1990s ("higher volume") Heavy Duty vs. the Ideal/Consolidated
   routines documented above.**
   - Multiple secondary sources (Art of Manliness #30, The Barbell #29) note
     that Mentzer's *earliest* Heavy Duty (1970s-80s, e.g. what he coached
     Yates on some accounts, and his own competitive training) used more
     exercises per session (several isolation + compound movements per body
     part, still one hard set each) than the ultra-minimal Ideal/Consolidated
     versions from the 1990s. `styles.json`'s current `heavy-duty` entry
     already captures something close to the *early-to-mid* version via its
     4-exercise-day structure but blends language from the later "Ideal
     Routine" naming. This project has not independently sourced a Tier A
     description of the *earliest* (1970s/pre-Ideal) Heavy Duty split in this
     pass — see Gaps.

## 5. Gaps — what we cannot honestly claim without more primary material

- **No independent verification of the original books' full text.** *Heavy
  Duty* (1980), *Heavy Duty II: Mind and Body* (1996), and *High-Intensity
  Training the Mike Mentzer Way* (2002/2003) are all real, citable, Tier A
  published works, but this research pass did not have access to their full
  text (out of print / paid / physical). Any claim attributed to "the book"
  specifically (as opposed to the mikementzer.org articles/videos) should be
  treated as unverified until someone can check an actual copy — this
  directly affects the Workout-B conflict in §2.3.
- **The frequency conflicts in §2.1 and §2.2 are not resolvable from the
  material gathered here.** The estate's own site contains at least three
  different cadences for the Ideal Routine and three different rest-interval
  claims for the Consolidated Routine. Without a single canonical, dated,
  primary transcript that supersedes the others, any `styles.json` copy must
  either state a range or explicitly flag the variance (as `blood-and-guts`
  does for weekly calendar order) rather than pick one number silently.
- **No verified Tier A source for the *earliest* (1970s/pre-"Ideal Routine")
  Heavy Duty split** — the version Mentzer used/taught before he arrived at
  the ultra-minimal 1990s Ideal/Consolidated routines. Secondary sources (Art
  of Manliness, The Barbell) reference it existing and being higher-volume,
  but neither is a primary citation with a specific magazine issue or book
  page. If `styles.json` ever wants to distinguish "early Heavy Duty" from
  "Ideal Routine" from "Consolidated Routine" as three distinct historical
  stages (which would be the most historically accurate framing), more
  primary digging (old Iron Man magazine "Heavy Duty" column archives, if
  they can be found/verified) would be needed.
- **No archived original Ironman magazine "Heavy Duty" column text was
  located or verified.** Mentzer wrote a monthly "Heavy Duty" column in
  *Ironman* magazine until his death in 2001 (per AbeBooks' description of
  book #22), which would be an excellent Tier A primary source for
  period-specific claims, but no accessible online archive of these columns
  was found in this pass.
- **The HITuni "Question and Answer Book" and "36-page presentation" (source
  #19) are paywalled course exclusives.** They are described as containing
  Mentzer's own answers to practical training questions "never before
  published," which would resolve several of the conflicts above, but this
  research pass could not access their contents (behind the paid HITuni
  course).
- **No claim in this document should be read as confirming Mentzer's
  approach is scientifically validated for natural (non-steroid-using)
  trainees at the same degree the mikementzer.org marketing copy asserts.**
  Multiple mikementzer.org articles claim "modern science" and specific
  studies (EMG research, a "seven-year, 14,000-participant" single-set study,
  myostatin/myokine research) "confirm" Mentzer's theories. None of these
  studies were independently checked or cited with a real reference in this
  pass — they are marketing framing from the estate's own promotional copy,

## 6. Suggested summary / principles / guidelines — DRAFT (Tier A/B grounded only)

**This is a draft for a future editing pass, not a finished replacement.**
Marked clearly as DRAFT throughout. Follows the existing `styles.json` schema
shape. Numbers below are taken only from Tier A sources cited above; every
place where the source material itself conflicts, the draft says so instead
of picking a number silently (per methodology rule #4).

> DRAFT `summary`: "Mike Mentzer's Heavy Duty, as documented in his
> estate-authorized 'Ideal Routine' presentations (mikementzer.org / Heavy
> Duty College): a four-workout rotation — Chest & Back, Legs, Delts & Arms,
> Legs — with exactly one working set per exercise taken to true momentary
> muscular failure, often via pre-exhaust supersets. Mentzer's own materials
> give the rest interval between workouts as either 'every third day' or
> '96 hours (four days)' depending on the article; both are cited here as
> approximate rather than a single fixed number. In his final years Mentzer
> taught an even more minimal 'Consolidated Routine' (two alternating
> workouts: Squat+Pulldown / Deadlift+Dips, one set each, roughly once every
> 5-10+ days) as the logical endpoint of the same theory — treat that as a
> distinct, later-stage program, not a variant of the Ideal Routine."

> DRAFT `principles` (all traceable to Tier A):
> - "Intensity, not volume or frequency, is the stimulus for growth; recovery,
>   not the workout itself, is when growth happens." (sources #3, #5, #10)
> - "One set to true momentary muscular failure is not a compromise — Mentzer
>   taught trainees to justify adding a second set only after the first
>   failed to produce results, not the reverse." (source #8, direct quote)
> - "As you get stronger, the same routine becomes a bigger stress, so
>   required rest increases over time — insert extra rest days as strength
>   climbs, even if that means training only once every 7-10+ days
>   eventually." (sources #5, #9)
> - "Pre-exhaustion (isolation set immediately into a compound set, no rest)
>   ensures the target muscle, not a smaller assisting muscle, is the one
>   that reaches failure." (sources #9, #12, #13)
> - "Judge progress only by whether you were stronger (more weight or more
>   reps) than last time on the same exercise — not by pump or soreness."
>   (sources #5, #9)
> - "Heavy Duty was not one fixed program — Mentzer prescribed different
>   set/frequency combinations (e.g. 4x/week at 6 sets, vs. 1-2 sets every
>   3-4 days, vs. 2 total sets once every 7 days) depending on the individual
>   trainee's recovery capacity." (source #19 — flagging this explicitly
>   would be new to `styles.json` and is arguably the most important
>   corrective to how the whole style is currently framed as one routine)

> DRAFT `guidelines` deltas vs. current `styles.json`:
> - `trainingDaysPerWeek`: current text ("About twice a week, train roughly
>   every 4th day") is defensible but should probably say "reported as
>   roughly every 3rd to every 4th day across Mentzer's own materials — not a
>   single fixed number" to honestly reflect the §2.1 conflict.
> - `workingSetProtocol`: current text is already close to correct; DRAFT
>   addition: name the Consolidated Routine explicitly as a separate,
>   later, even-more-minimal program (Squat+Pulldown / Deadlift+Dips,
>   1 set each) rather than leaving `styles.json` implying the 4-workout
>   Ideal Routine is the only official Heavy Duty structure.
> - `repRanges`: source #5's table (§4 above) supports splitting out "Chest
>   pre-exhaust compound follow-up" as 1-3 reps specifically (distinct from
>   general "Compound presses (post-exhaust)" already in `styles.json`,
>   which is close but could cite the exact pec-deck→incline pairing).
